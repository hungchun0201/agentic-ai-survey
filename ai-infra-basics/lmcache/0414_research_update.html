Research Update — LMCache Bottleneck & Predictive Prefetch Roadmap

Advisor sync, 2026-04-14: where LMCache pays off, where it collapses, and how bidirectional predictive prefetch can replace it.

Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct 8-turn agent, Poisson JPS H200 / H100 / A100 / L40S PACE + Lab 5090

1. Context & KV-Cache Strategies

We measure how three KV-cache policies behave across GPU memory profiles and load levels, to decide where LMCache's CPU offload tier is profitable and where a predictive prefetch path should replace it.

1.1 The four strategies we are comparing

The timeline below (from kv_strategies.html) shows the VRAM/DRAM lifecycle of each strategy during a single agent tool-call gap. Toggle between short tool (< TTL) and long tool (> TTL) to see where each approach breaks.

FCFS is the vLLM baseline. Continuum pins the job's KV in VRAM for the gap. LMCache offloads to CPU DRAM. Predictive Prefetch is the direction this work is moving toward.

2. Avg JCT vs JPS — Large vs Small DRAM

Each chart shows average job completion time (log scale) against arrival rate for H200 and H100. The contrast between large-DRAM (400 GB) and small-DRAM (64 GB) is the central finding: DRAM size gates whether LMCache's offload tier is ever profitable.

2.1 Large-DRAM sweep (400 GB DRAM)

Large-DRAM takeaway

With 400 GB DRAM, H100 (80 GB VRAM) is LMCache's sweet spot: −31% at JPS=3, −34% at JPS=6 vs Continuum. H200 (141 GB VRAM) has too little eviction pressure to amortize the offload cost — LMCache narrowly wins only at JPS=6 (−4.2%). Both GPUs collapse at JPS=15 where PCIe saturates.

2.2 Small-DRAM sweep (64 GB DRAM, default)

Small-DRAM takeaway

With only 64 GB DRAM, LMCache is slower than FCFS at every JPS level on every GPU tested. At JPS=10 on H200, LMCache hits 516 s while FCFS is 453 s and Continuum is 126 s (4× gap). The DRAM tier is too small to retain hit-worthy KV across jobs under concurrency — wait_for_save overhead pays in, cache hit rate does not pay out. The large-DRAM sweep is what reveals LMCache's real-world regime.

2.3 Bottom line

Regime Winner Why
Small DRAM (64 GB), any GPU Continuum Offloaded KV is overwritten by next jobs before reuse; LMCache pays cost without cache benefit

3. Per-Turn Latency — Three Distinct Failure Modes

Large-DRAM results broken down by conversation turn. Each pair shows H200 (left) and H100 (right). The per-turn shapes expose the structural failure mode of each policy — queuing growth (FCFS), admission spike then flat (Continuum), or flat then PCIe wall (LMCache).

3.1 JPS=3 (below saturation)

On H200 all three stay below 4 s/turn with minimal VRAM pressure. On H100, LMCache holds ~3 s throughout while FCFS climbs to 10.5 s by T7 — H100's 80 GB VRAM already creates enough eviction pressure at JPS=3 for LMCache's DRAM tier to pay off.

3.2 JPS=6 (saturation threshold)

Three structurally distinct shapes emerge. Continuum has a T1/T2 admission spike then stays flat (~5–8 s). LMCache is the flattest — its DRAM hit rate pays off. FCFS climbs linearly, hitting 34 s (H200) / 45 s (H100) at T7.

3.3 JPS=10 (high load)

Continuum's T3–T8 stay at 5–12 s while FCFS climbs to 72–88 s. LMCache sits in between but shows acceleration at later turns — the PCIe bus is starting to back up.

3.4 JPS=15 (fully saturated)

LMCache collapses: T5–T8 on H200 (106–149 s) now exceeds FCFS (104–137 s). PCIe bus saturation makes KV onload the new bottleneck. Continuum's T3–T8 stay at 5–14 s on both GPUs — pinned KV is structurally decoupled from system load.

3.5 Failure-mode summary

FCFS — linear queueing

T8/T1 grows with JPS (2.1× → 6.3×). Recompute of full context every turn; no structural ceiling. Unbounded linear growth.

Continuum — T1/T2 spike, flat tail

T8/T1 < 1 at JPS ≥ 6 (tail faster than first turn). Front-loaded admission contention, then pinned KV decouples from load.

LMCache — flat, then PCIe wall

Moderate T8/T1 at JPS 3–6 (2.1–2.3×), exploding to 10–11× at JPS=15. Bi-modal: flat below PCIe saturation, catastrophic above.

4. Next Steps — Roadmap Toward Predictive Prefetch

The results above define our problem statement. LMCache's reactive offload architecture has a structural limit at high concurrency (PCIe wall at JPS=15, 66 KV transfers/s competing for the bus). Continuum's pinned VRAM avoids PCIe but wastes capacity during tool gaps. Predictive prefetch is the synthesis: offload during tool execution, prefetch back just before tool completion. The roadmap below stages this into four verifiable phases.

Phase 1 Migrate from LMCache to SimpleCPUOffloading

Before we can build predictive prefetch, we need a much simpler offload substrate than LMCache. LMCache's connector architecture (per-step build_connector_meta, wait_for_save, per-layer onload) imposes structural overhead and is hard to extend with proactive hints.

  • Goal: port the working offload→reload pipeline onto vLLM's SimpleCPUOffloading connector, preserving the same hit-rate behaviour but on a substrate we can modify.
  • Deliverable: a reference implementation that matches LMCache's large-DRAM H100 numbers within ±5% at JPS 3–10, without the wait_for_save stall.
  • Verification: same 20260412 workload, same hit-rate, same wall-clock JCT — exposed behind a shim so later phases can swap in predictive logic.

Phase 2 Bidirectional PCIe + oracle-driven prefetch (correctness gate)

Once we own the offload substrate, build the predictive prefetch skeleton. In real agents VRAM is almost always full, so we need two simultaneous PCIe directions: evict KV we decide won't be used soon, and prefetch KV that will. To isolate the correctness question from the prediction question, we assume a perfect oracle for agent tool execution time.

  • Bidirectional PCIe: the scheduler issues offload (GPU→CPU) and prefetch (CPU→GPU) concurrently. Need to validate stream scheduling, memcpy coalescing, and that both directions actually run in parallel on the H100 (Gen5 x16 full-duplex).
  • Oracle assumption: we pretend we know the exact tool-return time. The prefetch scheduler uses this to trigger onload at t_return − δ_prefetch — just in time for the KV to be hot in VRAM when the tool result arrives.
  • Correctness check: verify the prefetched KV actually matches what would have been recomputed (bit-identical attention outputs), and that no VRAM leak or double-free occurs under concurrent offload+prefetch.
  • Success criterion: with oracle + correct bidirectional plumbing, zero wait on tool return → performance matches or beats Continuum at JPS 3–10, without pinning VRAM.

Phase 3 Scoring function — which KV to move?

Once Phase 2 proves the mechanism works with an oracle, the next challenge is the scheduling decision: which KV blocks should be offloaded to make room, and which should be prefetched back? This is a scoring problem. Our current design considers two factors jointly.

Benefit term

  • Saved prefill time — how many tokens of recompute we avoid on reload
  • Opportunity cost — if this KV stays pinned or pre-fetched, which other requests does it block?

Capacity term

  • VRAM headroom — how much eviction pressure is there right now?
  • DRAM capacity — our §2 finding is that DRAM size is the decisive variable determining whether offload/prefetch is effective at all

The score combines these to rank candidate KV blocks for eviction vs retention. The §2 small-vs-large DRAM gap (64 GB → 400 GB turns LMCache from always-loss into H100-JPS=6 win) tells us the capacity term is first-order, not a nuisance factor.

Phase 4 Real agent traces + imperfect tool-time estimator

The final step is removing the oracle assumption and testing on real workloads.

  • Real traces: replace the synthetic Poisson 8-turn benchmark with real agent tool-call traces (variable tool duration, heterogeneous turn counts, realistic mixing of tools).
  • Imperfect estimator: train a predictor for tool-execution time and measure how the scoring function degrades as the predictor's error grows. Questions we want answered:
    • How much prediction error can we tolerate before prefetch becomes net-negative (late prefetch = wasted PCIe)?
    • Is it better to prefetch slightly early (VRAM cost) or slightly late (recompute cost)?
    • Does the scoring function need to fold in the predictor's confidence?
  • Target: match or beat Continuum on real traces while using less VRAM (no permanent pinning), and beat LMCache on H100 JPS 3–10 by closing the wait-on-reload gap.
Why this sequencing

Each phase has a clean success gate that isolates one unknown. Phase 1 isolates the offload substrate (no prediction). Phase 2 isolates the mechanism (oracle prediction). Phase 3 isolates the scheduling policy (oracle timing, scored decisions). Phase 4 isolates the real-world robustness (no oracle). A failure in any phase points to a specific layer to fix, rather than an ambiguous end-to-end regression.
